Every experienced speaker in this community gave their first talk at a local meetup. That includes the people you see on the KubeCon stage. The path almost always runs through a room of thirty people, a bad projector, and a question you did not know how to answer.

That experience is not a step you skip on the way to the real thing. It is the real thing.

## The audience is rooting for you

Meetup audiences are not conference audiences. They are not evaluating you against a slate of competition. They showed up on a Tuesday evening, they are eating pizza, and they genuinely want to hear what you have been working on.

Nobody is live-tweeting critique. Nobody is comparing your slide design to the last talk. They want to learn something they can use, and they are already on your side before you say a word.

## You do not need to be an expert

The most useful talks at meetups are rarely from the deepest experts. They are from people who just figured something out — who went from confused to functional — and can remember what the confusion felt like.

> If you learned something in the last six months, you know things that people in that room do not. That is enough for a talk.

Expertise creates a kind of blindness to the beginner's experience. Recency does not. A talk called "How I finally understood Kubernetes Services" is more useful to most attendees than a talk called "Service Mesh Architecture at Scale."

## What to talk about

If you are stuck on a topic, start with one of these prompts:

- Something that took you longer to figure out than it should have
- A tool you started using that changed how you work
- A mistake you made in production and what you learned from it
- A concept you wish someone had explained differently when you were starting out

Any of those is a 20-minute talk. All of them are more valuable than a product demo.

## The practical benefit nobody mentions

Preparing a talk forces you to understand something properly. You cannot bluff your way through a live room. The gaps in your knowledge that you have been quietly ignoring become impossible to overlook when you are writing slides.

Most speakers report that they learned more preparing the talk than they had in the previous months of working with the technology. Teaching accelerates understanding. That effect starts with the preparation, not the delivery.

## How to submit

Cloud Native Valencia runs an open CFP for every event. We actively want talks from first-time speakers. If you are worried your topic is too basic, it is probably exactly right. Submit it.

The worst outcome is that we suggest some adjustments. The likely outcome is that you spend a month sharpening something you care about, stand up in front of your community, and walk away with a video you can point to for the rest of your career.

The room is already on your side. All you have to do is show up.
